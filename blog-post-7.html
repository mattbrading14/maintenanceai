How to Build a Vendor Directory
Before You Need One

MaintenanceAI Team · March 2026 · 5 min read

The worst time to find a plumber is when a pipe bursts at 10pm on a Friday. The worst time to find an electrician is when a tenant calls about a burning smell from an outlet. The worst time to find a locksmith is when a tenant gets locked out on a holiday weekend.

Every experienced landlord learns this lesson eventually — usually the hard way. The ones who've been managing properties for a while have a short list of trusted vendors for every trade, pre-vetted and ready to call. That list is one of the most valuable things you can build before you own your first investment property.

The Core Vendor Trades Every Landlord Needs

Regardless of property type or size, there are eight vendor categories that every landlord needs covered:

Plumbing
Leaks, clogs, water heaters, fixture replacement. Your most frequent call.
HVAC
Heating and cooling repairs, filter service, seasonal tune-ups.
Electrical
Outlets, panels, lighting, any burning smell or trip issue — licensed only.
Locksmith
Lockouts, rekeying between tenants, lock replacements.
General / Handyman
Doors, windows, drywall, fixtures, anything that doesn't require a license.
Pest Control
Annual inspections and treatment for rodents, insects, and termites.
Appliance Repair
Refrigerators, washers, dryers, dishwashers — if you provide appliances.
Fire / Life Safety
Annual extinguisher inspection, sprinkler systems, hood suppression.

How to Find Good Vendors

Ask Other Landlords First

The fastest path to a trustworthy vendor is a personal recommendation from another landlord in your area. Local real estate investor groups, neighborhood Facebook groups, and BiggerPockets forums are good places to ask. A vendor who has already proven themselves to someone in your network is far less risky than a cold Yelp search.

Use Nextdoor and Local Facebook Groups

For residential properties especially, Nextdoor recommendations from neighbors are often more reliable than online review platforms. People in your area have direct experience with local vendors and are usually candid about who to use and who to avoid.

Vet Before You Need Them

Once you have a name, do a small job with them before you need them for an emergency. Have the handyman fix a door. Have the plumber replace a faucet. See how they communicate, whether they show up on time, whether their invoice matches the quote. A vendor you've already used once is far more reliable in a crisis than one you're calling cold.

Confirm Licensing and Insurance

For licensed trades — plumbing, electrical, HVAC — always confirm the vendor holds a current license and carries general liability insurance before using them. Ask for a copy of their insurance certificate. This protects you from liability if something goes wrong during the repair.

A vendor directory isn't just a contact list. It's a pre-vetted, ready-to-deploy network that lets you respond to any maintenance issue within minutes instead of hours.

What to Record for Each Vendor

For each vendor in your directory, keep the following information on file:

  • Company name
  • Primary contact name
  • Phone number (including after-hours if available)
  • Email address
  • Trade / specialty
  • License number (for licensed trades)
  • Insurance expiration date
  • Notes — response time, pricing, quality, any issues

Keep Your Directory Current

Vendor directories go stale. Businesses close, contacts change, phone numbers get reassigned. Once a year, go through your list and confirm that each vendor is still in business and your contact information is current. The middle of an emergency is not the time to discover a disconnected number.
