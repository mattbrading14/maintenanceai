Why Your Tenants Shouldn't Have to Download an App to Report a Maintenance Issue

MaintenanceAI Team · March 2026 · 5 min read

Tenant portals are one of those ideas that sound great in a product demo and fall apart in real life. The pitch is compelling: tenants log in, submit a detailed maintenance request with photos, and you get a clean, organized ticket. No more messy text threads. No more voicemails you have to transcribe.

The problem is that nobody actually uses them.

Study after study on tenant behavior shows the same pattern: when tenants have a maintenance issue, they do exactly what they do when they need anything else — they text or email the person they know. Your phone number is in their contacts. Your email is on their lease. That's how they're going to reach you, whether you've set up a portal or not.

The Hidden Cost of Portal Friction

When you require tenants to use a portal to submit maintenance requests, you're adding friction to a process that directly affects the condition of your property. Think about what that friction actually means in practice:

  • A tenant notices a slow drain on a Tuesday night. Instead of texting you immediately, they think "I'll submit that through the portal tomorrow." By Thursday they've forgotten.
  • An older tenant or someone less comfortable with technology gives up on the portal after two failed login attempts and calls instead — creating the exact phone call you were trying to avoid.
  • A tenant dealing with a stressful situation — a leak, a broken heater in winter — doesn't want to navigate a form. They want to reach a human, fast.

Delayed reporting is one of the most expensive problems in property maintenance. A small leak that gets reported in week one costs a few hundred dollars. The same leak reported in week four — because the tenant kept forgetting to log into the portal — can cost thousands.

Every barrier between a tenant and a maintenance report is a risk to your property. Remove as many as possible.

The Setup Cost Nobody Talks About

Beyond tenant behavior, there's the setup side. Getting tenants onto a portal requires onboarding every new tenant when they move in, resetting passwords when they forget them (and they will), and explaining how the system works every time something changes. For a large apartment community with a full staff, this overhead is manageable. For an independent property manager handling 20 to 50 units alone, it's a real time drain.

You also have the adoption gap problem: even if you get 80% of your tenants using the portal, the other 20% are still texting and calling. Which means you're maintaining two workflows anyway — the portal and the phone — which is worse than just having one.

What Tenants Actually Do

Research on tenant communication consistently shows the same preferences:

  • Text message is the most common channel for maintenance requests among tenants under 50
  • Email is preferred by tenants who want a written record
  • Phone call or voicemail is still common for urgent issues and older tenants
  • Portal submission is used primarily when tenants have no other option — or when they're documenting something formal like a lease violation

Your tenants aren't going to change their behavior because you bought new software. The software needs to meet them where they already are.

A Better Approach

Instead of trying to change how your tenants communicate, change how you process what they send you. When a tenant texts a maintenance issue, screenshot the thread and upload it. When they email, screenshot the email. When they leave a voicemail, most phone systems generate a transcript automatically — screenshot that too.

Your tenants keep communicating the way they already do. You stop manually processing every message. Everyone wins.

The Bottom Line

Tenant portals were built to solve a problem for large property management companies managing hundreds or thousands of units with dedicated staff. For independent property managers and small landlords, they create more problems than they solve.

The best maintenance workflow is the one that creates the least friction for your tenants — because the faster they report issues, the faster you can fix them, and the less those issues cost.
