How to Write a Monthly Owner Report Without Spending Half Your Sunday on It

MaintenanceAI Team · March 2026 · 6 min read

If you manage properties on behalf of owners, monthly reporting is part of the job. It's also one of the most time-consuming tasks property managers face — especially when you're doing it manually at the end of every month, trying to piece together what happened from text threads, emails, and memory.

This post covers what a good owner report looks like, what it should contain, and how to produce one in minutes instead of hours.

What Property Owners Actually Want to Know

Before you can write an efficient report, it helps to understand what owners are actually looking for. Despite the variety in ownership styles, most property owners want answers to the same basic questions:

  • Is my property being maintained? What issues came up this month, and were they handled?
  • Is anything still outstanding? What's pending, and what's the status?
  • Am I on top of scheduled maintenance? When are the next major service appointments?
  • Are there any costs I need to know about? What did repairs cost, and what's coming up?

A good owner report answers those four questions clearly and concisely. It doesn't need to be long. A single page — or even less — is often better received than a multi-page document that owners don't have time to read.

The Structure of a Good Owner Report

1. Overview (2–3 sentences)

A brief summary of the month. How many maintenance issues came in? Were they routine or significant? Is the property in good shape overall? This gives the owner the headline before they read the details.

2. Completed work

A list of maintenance tasks that were finished this month, with the vendor, the date completed, and the cost if relevant. Keep it factual and brief — one line per item is usually enough.

3. Pending items

Anything currently in progress or awaiting vendor response. Owners appreciate transparency here — they'd rather know a repair is pending than be surprised by it later.

4. Preventive maintenance

Upcoming scheduled maintenance items — HVAC service due dates, annual inspections, filter changes. This shows the owner you're managing proactively, not just reactively.

Here's what this looks like in practice:

Sample owner report — March 2026

Overview: Three maintenance requests came in this month, all routine. Two have been completed; one is awaiting vendor scheduling. Property is in good condition heading into spring.

Completed work:
WO-4821 — Leaking pipe under kitchen sink, Unit 204 — Completed March 16 — Mario's Plumbing — $285
WO-4822 — Broken door handle, Unit 101 — Completed March 19 — Tri-City Lock — $120

Pending:
WO-4823 — HVAC not cooling, Unit 308 — Vendor contacted March 22, appointment scheduled for March 28

Preventive maintenance:
• HVAC filter change — Due April 1 — O.C. McDonald
• Annual fire extinguisher inspection — Due September 2026 — Nickell Fire Protection

Clean, clear, professional. Takes about 30 seconds to read. Shows the owner everything they need to know.

The best owner report is the one that gets read. Keep it short, factual, and consistent every month.

Why Reports Take So Long (And How to Fix That)

The reason monthly owner reports eat up so much time is that most property managers aren't logging maintenance in real time. Work gets done, but the record lives in a text thread, an email, a vendor invoice, and maybe a note on your phone. At the end of the month, you have to go find all of that and piece it together.

The fix is to create work orders at the time of every request — not as a month-end exercise. When every job is documented as it comes in, the owner report becomes a simple export of what's already been recorded. There's nothing to reconstruct.

How Often Should You Send Owner Reports?

Monthly is the standard — and it's usually right. Quarterly is too infrequent for owners who want to feel informed. Weekly is too often for properties without significant activity. Monthly hits the right cadence for most single-property or small portfolio owners.

One exception: if a major issue comes up mid-month — a significant repair, a tenant emergency, anything that will result in a notable cost — notify the owner when it happens rather than waiting for the monthly report. Owners don't like surprises, especially expensive ones.

The Bottom Line

A good owner report doesn't have to be long or elaborate. It just has to answer four questions: what got done, what's pending, what's coming up, and what did it cost. If you're logging maintenance in real time, that report practically writes itself.
